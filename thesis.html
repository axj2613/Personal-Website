Thesis Project

  • Neuro-Evolution for Multivariate Time Series Anomaly Detection

  • This work provides novel, highly efficient methodology for multivariate time series anomaly detection through the innovative application of neuro-evolutionary methods. It extends the Evolutionary eXploration of Augmenting Memory Models (EXAMM) algorithm to co-evolve asymmetric encoder and decoder components of autoencoder networks in a new algorithm called Evolutionary eXploration of Augmenting AutoEncoders (EXA-AE). The evolutionary process is performed at a fine-grained edge and node level, generating LSTM recurrent autoencoder networks with minimal compute requirements and number of trainable parameters. These networks are used along with a one-class support vector machine (OCSVM) to generate boundary thresholds, which identifies anomalies that outperform existing proven methods in terms of accuracy and cumulative F1 score, while at the same time requiring orders of magnitude fewer trainable parameters. By comparing the reconstruction error of model-produced forecasts, results suggest the fine-grained neuroevolution process helps evolve autoencoders which capture the most important features first, which in turn enables efficient and accurate anomaly detection. Results also show that the evolved autoencoders favor larger decoders, which can further inform efficient design decisions for future autoencoder network architectures.
